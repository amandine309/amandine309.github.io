The project

Building a machine that draws, hardware, firmware, and everything in between

This was a full individual project from MIT's course, build a functional 2D plotter from a component bag containing an Arduino Uno, stepper motors, a servo, belts, and assorted hardware. Everything else, the wiring, the firmware, the pen holder, had to be designed and built from scratch.

The plotter moves a pen across a sheet of paper in two axes using stepper motors and timing belts, lifts the pen between strokes using a servo, and interprets G-code-like commands to produce actual drawings. At the end: a physical machine that draws.

Everything I built
  • Designed and printed the pen holder in Fusion 360, with servo-actuated lift mechanism
  • Assembled the full mechanical structure : rails, belts, motor mounts
  • Wired the electronics : Arduino, two stepper drivers, servo, power supply
  • Wrote the full Arduino firmware : stepper control, G-code parsing, pen lift sequencing
  • Calibrated, debugged, and tuned until the drawings came out clean

Build process

Five phases, one machine

01
3D design in Fusion 360
The course provided a base design but the pen holder with servo lift had to be designed from scratch, attached to the existing PenHolderSlider without replacing it. The constraint: the pen distance to the slider must stay exactly the same as the non-lifting design.
02
3D printing & post-processing
Printed all parts in PETG. The critical step was post-processing, parts need to slide smoothly on the rails with minimal friction but no wobble. Too tight and the motors stall. Too loose and the drawings are imprecise. Used sandpaper to tune the axle holes until the carriage moved freely but without play.
03
Mechanical assembly
Assembled the full frame, mounted the stepper motors, and tensioned the timing belts. Belt tension is a delicate balance, too loose and the carriage skips, too tight and the motors can't drive it. The spec: about 5mm of play when pressing the belt with a finger. Every M3 screw had to be the right length.
04
Electronics & wiring
Wired the Arduino Uno to two stepper motor drivers (one per axis), the servo for pen lift, and the power supply. Stepper drivers need careful current calibration, too much current heats the motors and wastes power, too little causes missed steps. The wiring had to be clean enough not to snag the moving carriage.
05
Firmware & calibration
Wrote the Arduino firmware from scratch: G-code parser (M commands for pen lift, G0/G1 for moves), stepper step sequencing with acceleration, coordinate system management, and servo control for pen up/down. Calibrated steps-per-mm for both axes by measuring actual vs commanded distances, then iterated until drawing output matched input geometry.

What was hard

Three problems that took the most time

Ⅰ
The pen holder servo lift
Designing a pen holder that lifts cleanly is harder than it sounds. The servo needs enough torque to lift against the pen's weight, the lift distance needs to be consistent (too little and the pen drags between strokes, too much and re-engagement position varies), and the whole assembly can't add too much weight or inertia to the carriage. Multiple design iterations in Fusion 360 before getting a version that worked reliably.
Ⅱ
Stepper motor calibration
Stepper motors are open-loop, they don't know if they actually moved or missed a step. Getting the steps-per-mm ratio right required iterative measurement: command a 100mm move, measure actual distance, adjust, repeat. Any accumulated error means the drawing drifts from the intended geometry. The X and Y axes also have different gear ratios so each had to be calibrated independently.
Ⅲ
G-code parsing and trajectory planning
Writing a G-code interpreter on a microcontroller with limited memory requires careful design. The parser reads commands character by character, extracts coordinates, determines move type (rapid vs draw), and converts to step counts. Coordinating both axes simultaneously so the pen moves in a straight diagonal, rather than a staircase, requires computing the step ratio and interleaving steps from both motors correctly.

Takeaways

What a solo hardware project teaches you

01
Physical tolerances are real. A 0.2mm error in a 3D-printed hole means the axle binds. Unlike software where a bug produces a clear error, a mechanical problem produces mysterious motion artifacts that could be friction, backlash, missed steps, or belt tension, debugging requires physical intuition.
02
Hardware and software are deeply coupled. The firmware step count depends on the mechanical gear ratio, which depends on the 3D printed pulley diameter, which depends on the print quality. A change anywhere in the chain requires re-calibrating everything downstream.
03
Iteration is the process. First pen holder didn't lift cleanly, redesign. First belt tension was too tight, loosen. First firmware had staircase diagonals, fix step interleaving. Each cycle took hours. The final result worked precisely because of the iterations, not despite them.
04
There is something uniquely satisfying about a machine that makes physical things. Watching the plotter trace a clean circle on paper, a circle defined by coordinates in a text file, translated by firmware, executed by motors, rendered in ink, bridges the digital and physical in a way that pure software never does.
